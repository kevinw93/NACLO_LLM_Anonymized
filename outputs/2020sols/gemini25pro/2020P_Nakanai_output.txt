### Analysis of Reduplication Patterns

I identified three primary patterns and one minor, complex pattern:

1.  **Pattern A: Prefixing Reduplication (C1V1V2-)**
    *   **Process:** A prefix is formed from the first consonant (C1) and a vowel glide or simplified vowel derived from the first two vowels of the stem (V1 and V2). The general form of the prefix's vowel is V1V2. This combination undergoes simplification in certain contexts: `ea` → `a`, `oa` → `a`, `ie` → `e`, `eo` → `o`.
    *   **Environment:** This pattern applies to bisyllabic words where the two vowels are different (V1 ≠ V2) and the second consonant (C2) is an obstruent (a stop like /p, t, k, b, d, g/ or a fricative like /s, v/) or is absent.
    *   **Examples:**
        *   `beta` (V1=e, V2=a; C2=t) → `b`+`ea`+`beta` → `ba`+`beta` → `babeta`
        *   `gove` (V1=o, V2=e; C2=v) → `g`+`oe`+`gove` → `goe`+`gove` → `goegove`
        *   `basi` (V1=a, V2=i; C2=s) → `b`+`ai`+`basi` → `bai`+`basi` → `baibasi`

2.  **Pattern B: Infixing Reduplication (C_lastV_penult)**
    *   **Process:** A syllable is infixed immediately before the final syllable of the word. This infixed syllable is formed from the final consonant of the word (C_last) and the vowel of the penultimate syllable (V_penult).
    *   **Environment:** This pattern is triggered by vowel identity in specific positions.
        *   For bisyllabic words: V1 = V2 and C1 is a fricative (e.g., /s, h/). In this case, the process simplifies to suffixing the final syllable.
        *   For trisyllabic words: Two adjacent vowels are identical (V1=V2 or V2=V3).
    *   **Examples:**
        *   `sasa` (V1=V2=a; C1=s) → `sa-sa-sa`
        *   `baharu` (V1=V2=a) → infix `r`+`a` (`ra`) before `ru` → `baha-ra-ru`

3.  **Pattern C: Final Disyllabic Suffixing**
    *   **Process:** The final two syllables of the word are copied and suffixed. For bisyllabic words, this is equivalent to full reduplication of the word.
    *   **Environment:** This acts as a default pattern for words not meeting the criteria for Patterns A or B.
        *   Words of four or more syllables.
        *   Trisyllabic words without adjacent identical vowels.
        *   Bisyllabic words where V1 = V2 and C1 is *not* a fricative.
        *   Bisyllabic words where V1 ≠ V2 and C2 is a sonorant (a liquid like /l, r/ or a nasal like /m/).
    *   **Examples:**
        *   `vigilemuli` (5 syllables) → `vigilemulimuli`
        *   `kirosi` (3 syllables, no adjacent vowel identity) → `kirosirosi`
        *   `ligi` (V1=V2=i; C1=l) → `ligiligi`
        *   `mari` (V1≠V2; C2=r) → `marimari`
